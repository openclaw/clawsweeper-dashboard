## Summary

This PR should stay open: centralizing app egress is directionally coherent, but the patch intentionally removes OpenClaw’s default private-network destination guard from model- and remote-controlled fetches whenever the managed proxy is not active, while also retiring existing operator controls. The protected maintainer label and the security-boundary product choice require explicit maintainer judgment, and the claimed Crabbox proof is not linked in an inspectable form.

## What This Changes

The PR adds a shared managed-proxy-aware app fetch transport, migrates web/media/plugin/skill download callers to it, and retires `tools.web.fetch.useTrustedEnvProxy` plus `tools.web.fetch.ssrfPolicy`.

## Best Possible Solution

Introduce the shared app transport without weakening defaults: retain destination guarding for model- or remote-controlled URLs whenever an enforced managed policy proxy is not active, preserve or migrate shipped operator intent safely, and prove both fresh-install and upgrade paths with linked real-run evidence.

## Reproduction Assessment

Yes, from source with high confidence: the new tests explicitly exercise successful localhost/private app fetches, and the transport only detects managed-proxy activation rather than requiring it. No live execution was needed to establish the changed policy semantics.

## Solution Assessment

No. A shared egress transport is a reasonable layer, but making the external proxy the sole private-network policy owner even when it is disabled is not the safest or narrowest migration.
